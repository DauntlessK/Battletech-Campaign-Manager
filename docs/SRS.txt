==   OVERVIEW   ==

1. INTRODUCTION

1.1 Purpose

The purpose of this web app is to help players manage / run their battletech classic campaign. The platform, built upon React, will allow players to build forces with battletech units,create campaigns, assign a force to a campaign and manage that campaign.

It is designed not only to assist in the normal chaos campaign management - which has other competitiors - it is primarily designed to bridge a gap between running a simplified campaign like the chaos system, and running a campaign on MekHQ. This middleground is designed to give a bit more interesting depth to a campaign while still being easy to maintain.

1.2 Scope

The Campaign Manager will:

• Registered users can add friends, create & customize forces of various units and pilots, and create / manage / and partipate in campaigns
• Facilitate the running of a campaign by managing the player's resources and unit's status persistance
• Allow a few various types of campaigns to suit player's desired depth / complexity
• Within a campaign, allow a player to log a battle and thus record damage to units and track outcomes as they relate to the campaign

Techstack:

• React front end + tailwind
• Backend Flask? REST endpoints + server rendering?
• MySQL db

Definitions and Abbreviations

Battletech: A tabletop game from 1984 played on hex-based maps with many different types of units, most usually with mechs

Classic: Only supported game-type by the app, uses BV to balance, game played on hexes, as opposed to Alpha-Strike which is played without hexes (rulers, etc) and PV to balance

Mechs / Meks: an armored combat vehicle of roughly humanoid shape, standing 8 to 14 meters tall and typically massing from 20 to 100 tons.

BV: Battlevalue - used to balance units

Player: A partipant in a battle or campaign

Campaign: A colletion of battles fought with the same, finite units with assets and consequences carrying over from one to the next

Force: A set of units and pilots to be used in a campaign, built around restrictions like BV limit and era

Combat team: A group of units within a force, usually organized into a lance (4 meks) or star (5 meks).

Company: The assumed force size for campaigns, though not required. 3 lances of 4 meks each. So 3 total combat teams and 12 total meks.

Warchest / WP: Abstract points used only in chaos campaigns to represent c-bills, reputation, luck, etc that the player uses as currency to buy and upgrade units, repair, etc.

Faction Quality Rating: Represents the faction's default unit quality - which influences the ease of repairs (and other things not really relevant to this campaign manager)

Tech Rating: A-F rating that represents a unit's or equipment's (components, weapons, etc) complexity and thus repair difficulty. A being primitive, D being succession-wars level, and on. This should be reversed - cam-ops describes A-F as A being primitive. Should reverse this so that F is primitive.

Availability Rating: A-F rating that represents a unit's or equipment's (components, weapons, etc) ease of requisition. At A, it is very common and easy to find, at F, it is near-impossible. X represents unavailable in that time period. All items are given 3 availabilities such as C/D/B representing Star League era, Succession Wars and Clan Invasion availability.

4.0 CAMPAIGNS --------------------

Players partipate in one of 3 different campaign types with varying granularity / complexity. Each campaign has one of several settings that can be adjusted upon creation. They include:

Campaign Settings:
-Campaign type
-Campaign sub-type???
-Campaign Scoring
-Campaign era
-Rules Level
-Campaign Force BV limit
-[conquest only] Lances/Stars
-Victory Condition(s)

Some of these settings may be inherently decided by the campaign type selected.

4.1 Campaign Types --------------------

- CHAOS CAMPAIGN (2-10p) - The simplest option, an implementation of the chaos campaign system. Extremely simplified for quick and easy management and minimal admin and upkeep, the campaign uses a generic warchest currency to handle all rearm and resupply costs, unit purchases and sales, upgrades and more. The admin configures the campaign (setting things like BV / size, warchest size, era, fog of war, etc) and players battle at their own pace. After a battle, both players enter the results and the system will "marry" and verify the data as one battle entry (ensuring accuracy between the two records) that can be referenced at any time. Between battles players can spend warchest to repair, etc.

- ADVANCED CAMPAIGN (2-10p) - An advanced campaign is esentially a halfway point between a chaos campaign and the full campaign experience within cam-ops that is in MekHQ. It is designed to be grittier and more detailed than a chaos campaign but still be appraochable and managable within a faster timeline. Players manage cBills, individual pilot XP, part availabilty and time  (as a resource for repairs) The key concept is that units like mechs and such will bear true scars and players will truly feel like each battle is a part of a larger conflict.  Pilots may enter injured already, mechs may be missing armor or whole limbs / weapons, and more as unit attrition is front and center, making for engaging long-term struggle.

- CONQUEST CAMPAIGN (2p) - This strict 2p campaign takes the advanced campaign rules and adds a map and turn layer to it. Players fight over a planet with a full hex-map, which starts with multiple objectives all hidden across the map. The campaign takes place over a number of turns (either a preset limit, or unlimited) that consist of multiple days. Each turn, players will prioritize a repair and select a target hex to scout / control etc. Forces may be able to scout more hexes, or devote more time to repair, etc. 

4.2 Campaign Scoring --------------------

-While the campaign type determines the depth of management that goes into the player's force- in terms of resources, repairs, and orders, the scoring determines how the campaign is run and how players are ranked / doing in their campaign and ultimately who wins and loses the campaign. 

Victory Conditions / scoring

- [Chaos, Advanced, Conquest 2P] Total # of battles
- [All Types] Surrender / Automatic Capitulation based on BV or resources (warchest, c-bills)
- [Chaos, Advanced Campaigns] Domination - Over 75% control of objective world / area
- [Conquest Campaigns] Control of key objectives across the world
- [Conquest Campaigns] Finding and eliminating the enemy force's base / dropship
- [Advanced Campaigns, Conquest Campaigns] Time / turns elapsed

As it relates to chaos and advanced campaigns, the campaign owner sets a number of objectives (1-20 objectives total) that are fought over during the campaign. The campaign owner is also responsible for setting / fine tuning (or using default values) for battles. At its most basic, every battle can be worth a specific percentage of control over an objective. In 2p+ campaigns this would also mean a loss in control for the loser as well as a gain for the winner. Depending on the type of battle (and maybe results / # of losses / margin?) the amount won and the amount lost may differ.

Certain objectives, with control over a threshold (75%?) may grant additional bonuses. Such as: An additional tech /team of techs (for repairs), a cash income (one time or after each battle), a radar or comms facility, etc

The campaign is ideally suited for company-strength, which is with 3 lances of 4 mechs each.

These combat teams are assigned orders as a whole, and for the duration of a turn, that combat team performs that order.

4.7 Turns / Time --------------------

A turn for advanced / Conquest campaigns can be configured to some degree. A turn represents a set amount of time, usually 1 to 5 days.

For advanced campaigns, a turn is conducted in between battles. Following a battle, a player can use his turn to repair, rearm, and spend resources for other things like upgrades, etc. The campaign's configured turn length in days will dictate just how much work can be done to repair, rearm and reinforce.

For conquest cmapaigns, a turn is a length of time in which each player will assign orders and those orders are carried out. Each lance/star is given an order like patrol, defend, attack, repair and it performs that action over that turn. (See section 4.9 for more details)

There are no turns or concept of time in chaos campaigns- battles are fought and then units are repaired as resources are spent between battles.

4.8 Objectives -----------------

Objectives represent key locations of strategic value that are worth fighting over. In chaos / advanced campaigns, the player that has the most control over an objective receives its bonus. At the start of each campaign, control over each objective is divided equally among players.

• Factory - Gives a +1 bonus for rolls against requisition
• Depot - Gives a +1 bonus for stock rolls
• Comms Array - Increases chances to gain additional hexes in orders by 5% (Conquest only) or non-conquest: boosts gains from battle victories by 5%
• City - Gives 50k c-bills per turn (chaos: gain additional warchest points)
• Fort - Defensive bonuses when attacked
• Repair Facility - Gives a +1 bonus to repair rolls when performed at a facility (not improvised repairs)
• Space Port - Gives a +1 bonus for delivery time rolls
• Medical Facility - Gives +1 bonus to on rolls for healing.

4.9 Chaos Campaign details --------------------

A chaos campaign uses warchest points / support points as resources for each player's force. Players use these to repair, promote, buy new units and more. There are no turns or concept of time. Players battle and the effects are shown afterward once the battle has been logged. Then players repair units, etc in between battles. Depending on the desired victory condition(s), the campaign will end (and a victor declared) after.

Warchest points are converted into support points for the purposes of repair etc. See cam-ops for details.

Unit requisition works exactly the same as other campaigns (see 4.14 for unit req).


4.10 Advanced Campaign Details --------------------

Players must juggle managing c-Bills and time/turns as the battle enemies and keep their forces from falling apart or running out of funds. In an advanced campaign, players will take a turn which consists of a battle, then a period of several days (as configured in the campaign) following the battle. In campaigns that consist of more than 2 players, this could mean that some players have played more than others so are technically several turns ahead. This I believe is a better design choice than to disallow games from being played. A campaign setting for max turns ahead (which can be disabled) should help keep things more or less even if necessary. 

4.11 Conquest Campaign Details --------------------

In this 2-player exclusive campaign, players vie for control over a hex map (this can be planet-wide or regional) over a set length of time, with players setting orders for their individual combat teams [lances]. Each turn, players set orders for combat teams, like repair, patrol, etc and once complete, end their turn. Battles may then be automatically created based on these orders. 

The conquest campaign can best be desribed as a strategic board game on top of the normal battletech tactical layer.

4.12.1 Conquest Orders ---

Seize Territory [not owned-hex] - For this turn, the combat team gains control of the hex selected, and potentially the 6 surrounding hexes (% chance for each*). Any hexes surrounding the target hex that has an objective is ignored (cannot control objectives this way) 
Attack Objective [not owned objective hex] - For this turn, attempts to gain control of an objective. May start a battle, especially if defended. Success increases with each surrounding hex controlled. Requires control of at least 1 hex adjacent to the objective not counting any hexes gained this turn.
Patrol [owned hex that borders enemy hex]- For this turn, the combat team extends its control while searching for enemies operating in this hex or POSSIBLY each surrounding hexes (% chance for each**). 
Recon [hex] - For this turn, the combat team probes into enemy territory, looking for targets of opportunity and attempting to deny bonuses from held objectives 
Defend [hex] - For this turn, the combat team thwarts any attempts at territorial control by the enemy in the hex selected, and 6 surrounding hexes. There's also percetnage chance to deny recon. 
Field Repair & Rearm [Owned hex] - For this turn, the combat team performs improvised field repairs which helps contribute to control percentage and defense, but is able to perform basic repairs at a penalty. 
Facility Repairs - For this turn, the combat team performs advanced maintenance and repairs and is not considered at all for combat. Reserve - For this turn, the combat team is kept powered down and out for various reasons (major damage, awaiting parts, healing pilots, etc)

*For percentages beyond the inital hex, the system should first roll 2D6 to find out how many hexes are gained. A 6-8 roll gains 3 hexes, a 5 or 9 gains 2 hexes, a 4 or 10 gains 4 hexes, a 2 gains 1 hex (the main hex), a 3 gains 5 hexes, a 11 gains 6 hexes and a 12 gains all 7 hexes.
**Results in preventing hex control loss nearby while extending control into enemy / unowned hexes with greater percentage chances.

4.13 Repair, Rearmament and Requestioning --------------------

A key element in the advanced and conquest campaigns is that repairs and rearmament is not immediate and sometimes is a balance between getting a unit "good enough" to fight, resulting in units deploying with imperfect stats (missing armor, limbs, weapons broken). These campaigns adhere closely to the cam-ops rules for repair, rearming and requisition of parts.

The system is simplified in terms of overall granular control - as far as repair and requisition goes. As opposed to MekHQ where the player determines every repair attempt (or mass repair), and is responsible for stocking the force, the player instead is responsible for giving broad repair policy at the combat team level and a simplified, automatic stockpile / req system.

4.13.1 Rearming ---

Rearming can only be accomplished by performing one of the two repair & rearm orders. It is assumed that the player's force has enough ammo of every type to conduct the campaign, so  any stockpile of ammo is not necessary. It's only a matter of actually reloading any units that require ammo. It requires 15 min worth of time and is an auto-success to reload one bin, as laid out in cam-ops.

4.13.2 Repair ---

For a given turn, a player's techs have a set amount of time to perform repairs. Behind the scenes (likely not a campaign setting but could be an advanced setting), players have a set amount of techs and a tech experience level. In MekHQ, this is granular for each tech, but for simplicity here, this will be essentially an "averaged" experience level across the tech force.

The player selects for his combat team one of the two repair options. They are detailed as follows:

Improvised Field Repair & Repair: Can only perform armor repair and rearmament. (Cannot repair components, replace limbs, components, weapons, etc) Follow cam-ops rules for field repair rolls / rules. The plus side of using this over the facility repairs is that the combat team can still contribute and react as a defensive recon force. While not partipcating directly, it can "alert" other forces on other offensive duties and help negate enemy orders near it.

Repair Facility: A unit under orders to repair via repair facility gets additional bonuses for repairing (more likely to succeed in each repair and thus can repair more things - see cam-ops) The unit is considered non-combat during this period and will not take place in any battles, etc.

In addition to what type of repair, the player also needs to select the repair priority:

-General Repair Priority (The head tech chooses- essentially a flowchart on damage repair starting with rearming all units, then repairing missing limbs, then any missing weapons, then broken     equipment, then armor, then structure- working through all units in the force. So it does not prioritize an specific unit, only the repair type.)
-Repair Armor & Structural Priority (Prioritizes getting full armor and structure to all units)
-Repair limbs & components Priority (Prioritizes more time/resource heavy intensive repair jobs such as limb repair and replacement)
-Repair weapons Priority (Prioritzes weapon replacement first, and armor second)
-Fully Repair Largest Units first (Prioritzes fully repairing the heaviest units first and down from there)
-Repair, scrounge and salvage (General repair that gives bonus to acquiring parts, but less overall time is devoted to repairs as compared to other options)

After that, based on those settings, repairs are performed given the turn length (in days) x 8 hours (work time for techs) x # of techs with additional modifiers as described in cam-ops 
(such as tech quality, complexity, where the repairs are performed etc).

4.13.3 Stocks and Requistion ---

The unit is assumed to have brought enough ammo for the campaign so no stocks for ammunition of any type is required. But for parts (weapons, limbs and components (mek leg, jump jet, etc)), there is first an intial roll to see if the unit happens to have it in stock. This will vary depending on the unit and the part.

Every part will have a rarity which represents the ease at which it can be made or found. This is between A and F. If not known, it is default C. This is also how likely the unit is to stock a given item.

2D6 rolls against rarity:
A items requires a 4+, B items requires 5+ C requires 6+, D requires 8+, E requires 9+, and F requires 11+

2D6 rolls for delivery time depends on rarity:
A items: (6+) This turn (available for immediate repairs), (4-5) Next turn, (2-3) 2 Turns
B items: (7+) This turn, (4-5) next turn, (2-3) 2 Turns
C items: (7+) This turn, (5-6) next turn, (3-4) 2 Turns, (2) 3 turns
D items: (8+) This turn,  (6-7) next turn, (4-5) 2 turns, (2-3) 3 turns
E items: (10+) this turn, (7-9) next turn, (5-6) 2 turns, (3-4) 3 turns, (2) 4 turns
F items: (11+) this turn, (9-10) next turn, (7-8) 2 turns, (5-6) 3 turns, (2-4) 4 turns

Order of operations:
1) Stock roll - Roll to see if the item in question is in stock. (2d6 against rarity roll)
2) Requisition roll - If not in stock, another roll for req. (2d6 against rarity roll)
3) Delivery time roll - If req is successful, system rolls for delivery time. 
4) If req is unsuccessful, will attempt to req items next turn.

Each turn, a combat team will make X amount of req attempts total. So at most, X items can be requisitioned per turn for that combat team. This depends on the orders of that unit:
Any attack / sieze type orders: 1 req
Defend: 2 req
Field repair: 4 reqs
Facility repair: 5 reqs
** Repair, scrounge and salvage repair priority gives +2 reqs and +1 to rolls against rarity. (No effect on delivery time)

Note that a bonus or penalty could be applied based on a campaign setting for the world that the campaign is taking place on. For example, a periphery world might make supplies harder to obtain, while a core inner sphere world might make things easier to obtain.

4.14 Unit Purchasing, XP Purchasing, upgrades --------------------

Purchasing new units follows the rolls against rarity table above, so every mek needs a rarity level. If it's not found, another attempt to find that mek can be made next turn. Otherwise it fails and the player can choose to purchase a different mek.

XP can be spent at any time to improve pilots. I believe cam-ops contains skills and costs for improving mekwarriors.

4.15 Health --------------------

Healing for pilots that are injured depends on their severity. Each turn, a pilot at level 1 or 2 injury has a chance to progress "down" one level.

Any level 3 and beyond injuries (6 representing death) requires that the pilot be unassigned to a combat force on the frontline, or his/her combat force be only performing facility maintenance or reserve orders. A level 3 will automatically move down to 2 on one week of facility maintenance or reserve orders. A level 4 and a level 5 requires a roll of 6+ and 8+, respectively, to move "down" all the way to 2 - again assuming the pilot's combat force is on facility maintenance or reserve orders. If a level 4 fails the roll, it moves down to level 4 automatically.

4.17 Battles -------------------

During chaos and advanced campaigns, battles occur at the end of a turn automatically. The time / turns will not advance otherwise.

For conquest campaigns, a single battle MAY occur between turns. First, there is cadence that is preset in the back end that will be configurable long-term if needed. If a battle just occured, for example, another battle after one turn is less likely but not impossible. After 1 turn, the likelihood of a battle triggering again following the next turn would be 33%, and after 2 turns it might go up to 50%, then 75%, then 90%, then 100% each following turn.

Additionally, there are modifiers that will influence this on top of the base percentages. If one combat force is in the area that another combat force is carrying out orders, the orders each combat force is carrying out will influence the percentage, making it more or less likely a battle occurs. -----ADDITIONAL NOTES / MISC-----

The web app is designed to facilitate several different methods of running a classic battletech campaign. Registered users can add friends, create forces consisting of various units and pilots, and create campaigns. The main purpose of the program is to do the heavy lifting for various level campaigns, from simpler ones to more advanced ones, taking care of unit management (keeping track of unit's damage, repairs, etc), XP and resource expenditure and more. All campaigns share some common features:

• Force management between battles (repair, rearmament, purchasing new/replacement units, XP expenditure)
• Battle logs (a history of all battles fought in the campaign, with results in each, keeping track of wins, losses, participants, kills, and more)
• Resource Tracking (warchest / cBills, etc)

This means that the system must have comprehensive unit list, a clear UI to add units to build forces and edit and customize them. Creating and custimizing the unit to make it fielded like the user's very own unit, from name, unit insignia and TO&E, it should be easy to create the unit and build (add units, etc). 

It should be simple to create new campaigns and assign units, invite friends and manage the settings within. It should be clear how each campaign is run during creation. Below is a brief summary of the difference between campaigns.

- CHAOS CAMPAIGN (2-10p) - The simplest option, an implementation of the chaos campaign system. Extremely simplified for quick and easy management and minimal admin and upkeep, the campaign uses a generic warchest currency to handle all rearm and resupply costs, unit purchases and sales, upgrades and more. The admin configures the campaign (setting things like BV / size, warchest size, era, fog of war, etc) and players battle at their own pace. After a battle, both players enter the results and the system will "marry" and verify the data as  one battle entry (ensuring accuracy between the two records) that can be referenced at any time. Between battles players can spend warchest to repair, etc.

- ADVANCED CAMPAIGN (2-10p) - An advanced campaign is esentially a halfway point between a chaos campaign and the full campaign experience within cam-ops that is in MekHQ. It is designed to be grittier and more detailed than a chaos campaign but still be appraochable and managable within a faster timeline. Players manage cBills, individual pilot XP, part availabilty (Though simplified compared to cam-ops) and time (as a resource for repairs- also simplified). The key concept is that units like mechs and such will bear true scars and players will truly feel like each battle is a part of a larger conflict. Pilots may enter injured already, mechs may be missing armor or whole limbs / weapons, and more as unit attrition is front and center, making for engaging long-term struggle. - CONQUEST CAMPAIGN (2p) - This strict 2p campaign takes the advanced campaign rules and adds a map and turn layer to it. Players fight over a planet with a full hex-map, which starts with multiple objectives all hidden across the map. The campaign takes place over a number of turns (either a preset limit, or unlimited) that consist of multiple days. Each turn, players will prioritize a repair and select a target hex to scout / control etc. Forces may be able to scout more hexes, or devote more time to repair, etc.
